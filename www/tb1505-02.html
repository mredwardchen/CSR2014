Cascade Microtech addresses need for constant voltage EM testing

by Debra Vogler, Senior Technical Editor


Cascade Microtech, Inc. recently announced a new Multipurpose Electromigration (MPEM) module featuring an intuitive, full-featured test suite for predicting the lifetime and reliability of copper interconnects in modern integrated circuits. The company noted that new test techniques, such as constant voltage EM (CVEM), show great promise in complementing the traditional method of constant current EM (CIEM) stress. The MPEM Module provides a commercial EM test to support this new CVEM evaluation method. It also provides multiple stress bias types for testing copper interconnects, typically with geometries of 32nm or less. With MPEM, the company added CVEM and low-frequency pulsed current electromigration (LFPEM) applications to its existing lineup of constant current electromigration (CIEM) and stress migration capabilities.

According to Cascade Microtech, the driver for using CVEM is that it offers a “natural insulation” from increasing dimensional variation and stress bias corruption. Asked to comment on why the CIEM methodology is running out of steam, hence, the need for CVEM, Timothy McMullen, Director of Product Marketing, Reliability Test Products, told Chip Scale Review that, in addition to the shrinking EM reliability margins, there is increasing statistical uncertainty as process variability increases. “There is a challenge associated with using current density to stress a device under test (DUT),” explained McMullen. “Line cross sectional variation corrupts current density to cause an unpredictable shift in each individual DUT's failure time, and CIEM can dramatically overstress devices as they begin to fail.”

The overstress problem, according to the company, results in “Inappropriate void formation with multiple false failure events and unrealistic recovery mechanisms in scaled interconnects where barrier conductivity is dropping significantly (Ref.: “Constant Voltage Electromigration for Advanced Interconnects,” Cascade Microtech Technical Brief).

The technical brief explains that geometries and materials to maximize fractional conductor area and reduce copper resistivity point to increasingly resistive/non-conductive barriers, which changes the nature of DUT behavior. “To maximize fractional conductor area and combat surface scattering, manufacturers need to dramatically shrink the thickness of the barrier layer,” McMullen told CSR. “The thinner barrier layers that are being proposed will have a higher resistance. For example, manganese-based self-forming barriers will oxidize to become non-conductive.” Figure 1 illustrates electron flow in a traditional copper line as current density stress creates voids in the material, and is compared with the interconnect scheme of the future.

Figure 1: Current flow through copper lines as void form: a) Un-degraded line with normal electron flow; b) Today’s lines, after void forms: the flow splits to “shunt” through the barrier; and c) Lines of the future, after void forms: there is no barrier shunt to share the current, crowded flow.
