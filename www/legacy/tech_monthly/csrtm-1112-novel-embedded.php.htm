Novel Embedded Die Package Technology Tackles Legacy Process Challenges

Novel Embedded Die Package Technology Tackles Legacy Process Challenges
 
As Deca Technologies’ celebrates its first anniversary, the electronic interconnect start-up is poised to ramp its second product family to volume production.  The company claims this embedded die packaging technology, M-Series, is a game changer due to novel processes that solve issues like pattern misalignment, warpage, board-level-reliability, and yield that have plagued embedded wafer-level ball grid arrays (eWLB), reconstituted chip packaging (RCP), and several other embedded die packages.  

“While the industry buzz continues on 2.5 and 3D technologies, Deca made a strategic decision to focus on extreme miniaturization through a cost-effective embedded die solution that handset OEMs critically need.  We have seen multiple instances where our semiconductor customers are asking to shave tens of microns off the size of a device to meet an OEM’s requirement.” said Tim Olson, CEO of Deca.

The Die Shift Problem
According to Olson, embedded die technologies struggle with die shift and reliability issues for a number of process-related reasons.  First of all, die embedded in a panel are not perfectly aligned; typically, a die position accuracy of <10μm and rotational accuracy of <0.1° are required, which has necessitated slow and expensive pick-and-place tools to be used.    Additionally, ever-shrinking fab geometries and the associated IO density can result in potential yield issues.

Prior Solutions
Creating larger bond pads on the device is one solution to the die-shift problem, but this doesn’t work for advanced silicon nodes with fine pitch interconnect, said Olson, and may require an additional redistribution layer or custom final metal design.  Another alternative is using high-accuracy die placement equipment with localized alignment, which can be a slow and costly process.  Local alignment of stepper fields has also been tried, but this approach still needs high-accuracy die placement and can cause reduced throughput at exposure.  The previous alternatives, Olson cautioned, lead to an expensive and less than optimum process within a fan-out wafer level package (FOWLP) flow.

The Deca Approach – What’s Different?
Built on Deca’s unique wafer-level process inspired by the solar cell manufacturing techniques of one of its investors, SunPower Corp., this new class of panelized packaging first involves fully embedding die in a molded wafer or panel. This advancement creates a rugged package with a fully encapsulated die that experiences minimal warpage thanks to a balanced molded structure (Figure 1).  It also improves board level reliability as compared with typical fan-in WLCSP. Additionally, copper pillars can be placed either directly on the bond pad, or routed along the RDL (Figure 2). This approach reportedly achieves the smallest package form factor possible, with no custom substrate required.

Figure 1:  M-Series fully molded device structure

Figure 2:  Cross-sectional view

However, the most significant process advancement for the M-Series can be attributed to Deca’s proprietary Adaptive Patterning software technology, which features unique optical inspection and design capabilities to reportedly correct for die shift and recalculate device IO to ball interconnects on-the-fly.

As Olson explained, in developing the Adaptive Patterning concept, Deca was inspired by “an age-old solution” called wire bonding.  As part of a typical wire bond process, the precise die location is measured prior to commencing bonding.  Combining actual die location with fixed leadframe or laminate trace information, the route for each wire is calculated in real-time during manufacturing.
 
The flexibility of wire bond has made it the preferred interconnect solution for over four decades.  In recent years, performance and size constraints have finally pushed a large share of new devices into the flip chip format.  The inherent electrical performance benefits of flip chip are clear, yet the cost and inflexibility of the approach has held back adoption to mainly those applications requiring the technology.

Deca’s new Adaptive Patterning methodology includes a unique integration of capabilities which enable the flexibility of wirebond, yet result in the performance of flip chip.    The approach allows features such as vias and redistribution traces to dynamically align to shifting die within an embedded device structure. Through the unique combination of a fixed design pattern or “Prestratum” plus an adaptive region, the methodology resembles the characteristics of classic wire bonding, yet is realized through a wafer-level build-up flow. With the addition of a dimensional inspection step and processing through the company’s automated design software, Deca’s approach can create a unique design for every device within a molded panel, thereby removing the classic barrier of a cost-effective embedded flow (Figure 3).

Figure 3:  Adaptive Patterning allows for flip chip performance with the flexibility of wire bond

“M-Series is unique in the ability to deliver the performance of flip chip, while offering the flexibility of wire bond,” explained Olson. “Our structure and manufacturing approach break through the board-level reliability and cost issues that have held others back.”

The industry has responded favorably to the company’s first product family of wafer-level chip scale packaging (WLCSP) touted as the smallest, most cost-effective packaging solution available. However, it’s often not the right solution because the chips aren’t big enough for all the IO, or the chip is too big for direct board mounting.  Olson believes the M-Series can solve both issues; it provides the smallest form factor possible when devices must grow to support the required device IO and enhances board level reliability for larger chips.  The resulting package thickness starts at a slim 0.6mm mounted height with a roadmap to even thinner options. Additionally, the mold compound that surrounds the chip is a key element in mitigating stress from the motherboard and offering higher reliability than classic wafer-level packaging.

The M-Series joins Deca’s flagship fan-in Wafer Level Chip Scale Package (WLCSP) options in serving handset OEM’s drive to integrate more features and functionality into tomorrow’s handsets.

“We are pleased to offer this exciting high performance, miniaturized and cost-effective option for IC designers, product engineers and system architects; M-Series with Adaptive Patterning.” said Olson.  “We have a handful of alpha customers already engaged and expect to ramp the technology into volume production during the second half of 2013.”
