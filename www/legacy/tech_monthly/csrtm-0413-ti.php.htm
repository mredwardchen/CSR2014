Small Die, Growing Problem

by Todd Wyant [Texas Instruments]

Semiconductors shrink in size each year, while the challenge of Moore's law further drives the shift to smaller sizes down the manufacturing line. Table 1 represents the standard analog formats for chip size over the years. The trend shows approximate timing and advancement in die size reduction. We are quickly approaching being invisible to the naked eye: within the last few years, we have developed chips smaller than the typical size of an ant.

Assembly processing challenges are starting to reach critical mass as the shrinking die size continues on analog products. The problems of small, cheap components are leading to cost increases in backend processing, placing customer surface-mount technology (SMT) lines and delivery methods in question due to handling of these small footprints. This article will review key challenge areas, including wafer dicing, tape and reel, wafer mapping, packing materials and SMT feeder.

Table 1: The standard analog formats for chip size have decreased over the years, quickly approaching to sizes that are near invisible to the naked eye.

Wafer Dicing: Where the Challenge Begins

The challenge of small die processing begins at the wafer dicing process. Wafer dicing is the act of mechanical singulation of the individual semiconductor parts. Today, dicing saws are capable of processing 300mm wafers, but as the die sizes approach 1mm on a side, the complications being encountered during singulation are die fly, bond pad corrosion, and increases in topside/backside edge damage.

Die fly is a common dicing challenge that has been dealt with for years. Die fly occurs during singulation of the individual circuits by the mechanical saw. The chips come off of the wafer dicing tape and wash away due to lack of adhesion with the backside of the chip. With parts reaching 0201 chip size we have reached the area where surface bonding area of the tape is not enough to overcome the dicing forces. The challenge is developing new adhesives that will hold die during dicing and enable further reductions in die fly. New tapes are driving the success at removing die fly. They have high adhesive strength with low removal effort needed post-dicing when utilizing a UV release process.

Even with the implementation of new tapes for die, fly on edge die remains a problem. Blade manufacturers address this problem through multiple flange designs that enable chip deflection away from the dicing area to prevent blade breakage. It is critical that in conjunction with new dicing tapes, a suitable blade flange design is chosen to combat edge die fly issues that remain. In addition to these items, equipment upgrades for chip collection need to be implemented to prevent damage to dicing equipment related to accumulation of chips in the dicing saw, which creates water leakage paths on the machines. These are all critical lessons learned for combating die fly issues with small die sizes.

The next compounding factor of die fly is topside/bottom side edge chipping increases associated with small die. The reduced backside bonding area for the dicing tape that enables die fly also enables increased sensitivity to vibration forces during the dicing process. The combination of die wanting to lift and smaller cutting area leads to increased chipping on both the top side and bottom side of wafers during dicing. Using new dicing tapes that have thinner and harder adhesive materials is one approach to enabling less vibration and die lift.

On the equipment front, multiple new non-mechanical dicing processes are now available that will address chipping and traditional dicing challenges. Laser dicing, water jet dicing, and dicing before the grind process have enabled ways of addressing fundamental dicing challenges with small die. As these new processes become mature, the hope is that they will develop into cost competitive replacements for mechanical dicing.

The final dicing challenge encountered is also addressed with new dicing technologies and this problem is bond bad corrosion. Bond pad corrosion is occurring at an increasing level due to wafer contact with dicing water and silicon slurry during processes that are approaching 14 hours per wafer in some cases. With existing mechanical dicing, the problem is addressed with the use of surfactants as well as selective use of cleaning water during dicing that limits the exposure level of wafers with both the saw slurry and volume of deionized water.

Die Attach, Tape and Reel Challenges

The next major assembly challenge facing assembly is both die attach and tape and reel processing. Die attach challenges are very similar to the issues facing tape and reel in that picking these small components requires custom tooling and complicated set-ups combined with high precision vision detection to enable touchdown of the pick collets to the pick stage below the wafer. The industry has been able to keep pace with kits and collets available now to enable down below 1005 chip sizes for the pick process, but very high wear rates increase processing costs and process maintenance.

Once picked, the two processes of die attach and tape and reel differ to a great degree. At die attach, the issues lie in how to enable placement during the epoxy dispense drying process without voiding and not wetting. This has been addressed with spin coat, and laminated tape applied die attach adhesives that are heat activated, but these have further complicated the assembly processes in that they create additional dicing and pick challenges as well as higher end cost of processing. For epoxy dispense, multiple head dispense systems have enabled process capability on current lead frame densities, but have reached the process window on super wide lead frame designs and is currently being worked as an open problem.

Tape and reel processing has yet to enable a cheap and suitable solution for small die applications. As die sizes approached 0201 formats early last year, the traditionally used polycarbonate type carrier tapes approached 75% of backend assembly costs and created additional cost pressures for delivery. The current solution is to advance from 4mm to 2mm pitch tapes to cut cost in half, but low cost tape has not been enabled to this point. Paper tapes allow a much lower cost point, but do not have the adequate pocket hole to enable use for these small components at this point in time. Other options of coverless type carrier tapes require even higher cost points.

As parts are approaching 1005 and below, the carrier tape forming tolerances are becoming the major obstacle since tape and reel equipment placement tolerances and carrier tape forming tolerances do not allow the dimensionality to meet EIA481 guidelines for processing. A movement is needed in the industry to improve SMT feeder capabilities to enable smaller die pick capabilities. The current sizes have reached a wall in both mass and size and the current equipment and tape capabilities will not bridge the gap needed to carry us into future successful delivery to end users without a major drive in this area.

Die tomb stoning and die tossing (Figure 1) have reached the customer line yield loss threshold for these small components and the problem is only going to accelerate as the part size and mass become smaller.

Figure 1: a) Die tomb stoning and b) die tossing have reached the yield loss threshold, but the problem will only accelerate if left unaddressed.

Surface mount (SMT) advances are needed for both part containment in the pocket during indexing and also for 2mm indexing and sub-2mm indexing of components. These feeder advancements have been worked, but end user adoption is still needed to drive fixes to fundamental die delivery problem resolution.

As die sizes become smaller, we must address fundamental challenges facing backend package assembly processing. For many years, we have gone without significant technological improvements in the capabilities to keep assembly cost in line with reductions in silicon costs associated with part size reductions. Many problems must be addressed today to enable lower cost solutions to past problems that have resurfaced with the ever shrinking part sizes.
