Solar-Cell Packaging

Wenchao Li, Seungcheol Back, MiHee Ji, Soyeon Jeong and Rao Tummala Georgia Institute of Technology, Atlanta, Ga.

I. INTRODUCTION

SOLAR CELLS have been extensively explored as a way to efficiently harvest the energy from the sun and thus revolutionize the energy industry in a different direction than in the past.. The advantages of solar energy compared with other forms of energy are that it is free, essentially unlimited and is not localized. Solar cells are able to directly convert sunlight into usable electricity without fuel availability, pollution, waste, or heat disposal problems. Solar cells operate based on the photovoltaic effect, photon energy is absorbed by the solar cell from the sunlight, generating trillions of electron-hole pairs which are routed to a p-n junction, where they are separated by an electric field and collected by contacts to provide electricity to the load. Of the different types of solar cells currently used, single crystalline silicon solar cells are the oldest and dominate the market most, as well as the most efficient. Other types of solar cells include polycrystalline, multi-crystalline, amorphous or thin film, and organic polymer-based cells.

One of the most important factors as to why solar cell technology is not widely adopted is the high cost of the solar cell system per unit of power. Therefore, for the systems to be widely used, the cost of production needs to be reduced. Ways to reduce the cost of solar cell systems include increasing the efficiency of solar cells, smart integration of solar cells into buildings, and increasing the lifespan of cells to offset some of the initial setup costs. Solar cell systems are comprised of solar cells and the solar cell packaging. According to the National Renewable Energy Laboratory, solar cell packaging accounts for 50% of the entire system cost [1]. Thus, it is important for solar cells to be efficiently packaged in order to lower the cost as well as to protect the solar cells from dust, wind or humidity damage. A well-packaged solar cell system will also increase the solar cell's lifetime and protect users from electrical shock.

Fig 1. Typical solar cells packaging structure

Most solar cell packaging technologies consist of a transparent top surface, an encapsulant between the front and rear sheets, solar cell interconnections and a frame. This article is a review of the material requirements for solar cells as well as other considerations that need to be taken into account in packaging solar cells from a mechanical, material, chemical, and electrical standpoints.

II. Packaging structure

A. Front Sheet

The front surfaces of solar cell packages serve several purposes including providing mechanical strength and rigidity to support the solar cells. In addition, they provide impact resistance, optical transparency, electrical isolation of the cell circuit and outdoor durability [2]. The front sheet must have high transmission to wavelength from 350nm to 1200nm as well as low reflectivity. Reflection can be reduced by applying an anti-reflection coating to the front surface. Alternative techniques to reduce reflection include "roughening" or texturing the surface. However, this process often causes dust and dirt to attach itself to the top surface. Lastly, the front sheet should be stable under prolonged UV exposure.

There are several choices for front sheet material including acrylics, polymers and glass. Tempered, low iron-content glass is most commonly- used, as it is low cost, strong, stable, highly transparent, impervious to water and gases and has good self-cleaning properties [3].

B. Encapsulant

Encapsulants are used to provide adhesion between the cells and the top and rear sheets of the solar cell packaging. The encapsulant should be stable at elevated temperatures and high UV exposures. It should also be optically- transparent and of low thermal resistance. Ethylene vinyl acetate (EVA) is the most commonly-used packaging encapsulant. The resistivity of the encapsulant is important for electrical insulation as low resistance of encapsulant materials has been linked to electrochemical corrosion [4,5]. The volume resistivity of several candidate encapsulant materials is shown in Figure 2.

Fig 2. Volume resistivity of several candidates

EVA is a co-polymer of ethylene and vinyl acetate. It is a polymer that approaches elastomeric materials in softness and flexibility and yet, can be processed like other thermoplastics. The EVA has good clarity, gloss, barrier properties, low-temperature toughness, crack resistance, hot-melt adhesive bonding, good water proof properties, and resistance to radiation. In addition, EVA has little or no odor and is competitive with rubber and vinyl products in many electrical applications. The chemical structure of EVA is shown in Figure 3.

C. Interconnections

Interconnections are needed to connect the individual cells within the system. They need to be connected in series or in parallel in order to get sufficient voltage and current for the required application. Therefore, to get reliable performance from cells, it is necessary to design and fabricate interconnections for thermal and mechanical stresses which come mainly from soldering processes. There are two soldering process steps; the first step is cell interconnections, called stringing or tabbing, and the second step is bussing. Initially, the cells are electrically connected using tinned copper ribbon and attached in a series, by precision placement onto a glass substrate and reflow soldering. Six to ten rows of cells are then "bussed" together using wide bus ribbons to create the collector grid.

The need to reduce the manufacturing costs is driving a steady reduction in wafer and cell thicknesses. As a result, the soldering process has become more challenging because thinner cells are more sensitive to thermal stresses. When exposed to high temperatures, over 250c, during the solder process, thin cells tend to warp and break much easier because of the difference in thermal expansion between silicon and the interconnection material. This thermal stress initiates micro-cracks and, in the worst case, causes open circuit failure.

One of the solutions to address or reduce the thermal and mechanical stresses is a stress-relief architectural approach. As each cells tends to expand when temperature goes up, the distance between each cell changes. To cope with this interconnect issue, a loop shape, as described in Figure 4, is proposed. In Z-shaped contacts, the contacts connect the back side of one cell to the front side of an adjacent cell.

Fig 4. Z-shaped contact with stress-relief Architecture.

Magnetic interconnections are also used in solar cell packaging. Establishing this kind of interconnection does not require heat as it utilizes two magnets to clamp the package [4]. Another promising solution is to use conductive adhesives, considered good for low-stress, as a lead-free alternative to solder interconnections. Recent developments in flexible conductive adhesives have greatly increased their reliability. Low process temperatures along with excellent mechanical properties, conductivity and durability lead to low-stress and reliable interconnections [5].

The most common interconnect material in solar cells, as in most packages, is copper. Copper is widely used for packaging interconnects due to its excellent electrical conductivity and low cost. However, copper has a huge CTE mismatch with common solar cell materials, especially silicon, which can cause severe mechanical failure as the cells absorb or produce heat. Conductive composites or alloys, such as tungsten and molybdenum, which have the same CTE as the solar cell substrate, have been used, but the electrical conductivity of these materials is inferior to that of copper [6].

D. Rear Sheet

The rear sheet in solar cell packaging serves a number of purposes. First, it provides physical protection to the module. The back needs to be resistive to punctures and abrasions. In addition, the back surface acts as a moisture barrier that also electrically isolates the cells and interconnects from the environment in which the panel is installed. Second, the rear sheet can actually enhance the module efficiency by providing reflection from the back surface to generate more power [7].

The key characteristics of the rear sheet of the solar cell packaging are that it must have a low thermal resistance and prevent ingress of water or water vapor. In most solar cell packaging, a thin polymer sheet, typically polyvinyl fluoride (PVF), is used as the rear sheet.

PVF has low permeability for vapors, burns very slowly, and has excellent resistance to weathering , staining and chemical corrosion. The chemical structure of PVF is shown in Figure 5.

F. Frame

The frame is the structural enclosure for the photovoltaic module. Photovoltaic frames are typically constructed from aluminum which is highly- resistant to external corrosive effects. If damaged during handling and installations, the aluminum will oxidize itself, creating a self-limiting aluminum oxide layer that is very resistant to corrosion.

III. FUTURE

A. Evolution of Solar cells

In 1839, a French physicist, Antoine Becquerel, discovered the photovoltaic effect while experimenting with a solid electrode in an electrolyte solution. He observed the creation of voltage when the electrode was immersed in the electrolyte solution upon exposure to sunlight. In 1883, Charlies Fritt constructed the first true solar cells using junctions formed by coating the semiconductor selenium with an ultrathin, nearly transparent layer of gold. In 1946, first doped- silicon was used in solar cells to increase energy-conversion efficiencies. By the late 1980s, silicon cells, as well as those made of gallium arsenide, with efficiencies of more than 20 percent have been demonstrated.

In 1989, the first dual- junction solar cell was created and, 4 years later, its usage began in the aerospace industry. In research, many advances are reported, such as 30% energy-conversion efficiency by 1995 and 40% energy-conversion efficiency by 2009 [8].

B. Future of the solar cells

Usage of fossil fuels such as coal, oil and natural gas to produce electricity dirties the air, consumes and pollutes water, harmfully- impacts plants and animals, and creates toxic wastes.. Solar cell energy production appears to address most of these issues. Two problems remain to be solved: cost and abundance. Cost is dominated by not only solar cells but also the packaging of these solar cells. With the rise in demand for clean energy and by massive investments by governments funding in Europe, China and US, the solar cell market has grown to $20billion in the last 6 years. In spite of this growth worldwide, solar energy makes up a very small fraction of U.S. consumption [9].

One of the trends in the future of solar cell applications is to utilize solar cell technology in energy- saving products, such as street lamps and ATM machines. As solar cell technology becomes cheaper, it will become an integral part of our lives, as shown in fig.6. As shown in this figure, solar cells could be embedded and packaged in our clothing or bookbags, which will enable us to charge our portable devices such phones, tablets or laptops anywhere we go.

Another future solar cell technology application is the development of the indoor solar cell systems.These systems are being conceptualized to generate electricity, based on the illuminaiton of fluorescent lights.

Fig 6.Embedded solar cell

With all of the above conceptual applications for solar cell technology , solar cell packaging is a major barrier for those concepts to become reality. Thus, there is a great potential in solar cell packaging research to reduce cost and size, and increase efficiency.

IV. CONCLUSION

In recent years, the solar cell industry has grown rapidly. As the industry continues to develop solar cell technology, packaging becomes the single biggest barrier which requires an innovative and a multidisciplinary approach involving electrical, mechanical, chemical, and materials- sciences. . Packaging will play an important role not only in passive protection of the solar cells, but also in actively enhancing efficiency.
