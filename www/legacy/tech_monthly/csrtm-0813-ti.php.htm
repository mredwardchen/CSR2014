Wide Lead Frames, Big Assembly Challenges

By Todd Wyant, Texas Instruments

Semiconductors shrink in size each year, while the challenge of Moore's law further drives the shift to smaller sizes during wafer fabrication to reduce silicon cost. The countermeasure during assembly and packaging is to increase strip content to generate more units per strip and to increase efficiency in output during packaging.

This article will discuss the issues encountered and equipment solutions for lead frame packages that continue to evolve, pushing the limits of strip density in production.

The current state of large lead frame

Lead frames are trending wider and approaching the limits of lead frame manufacturing capabilities from suppliers. They are produced from coil stock utilized in reel to reel type batch processing. Unfortunately, the roll size limits the strip width. This trend is progressing from starting sizes of 50x250mm and equipment advances in capabilities to 90x270mm. There are also many manufacturers working to push the trend to 100x300mm and beyond. Typically, 100mm wide inputs strips are split into two equal strip widths. But with recent increases in width, the waste material from the strips increases from the splitting process, so cost per unit of the lead frame may slightly increase as it approaches the max strip width.

The strip density for quad-flat no-leads (QFN) is greatly enhanced as we go wider (Fig. 1). As the industry progresses to single block mold, we can further increase strip density by another large factor as the empty tie bar areas of the lead frame will be filled with active components. Figure two shows the difference of unit count as we progress from four blocks to two block mold based on product package size on QFN. As we shift to one block mold, we can expect a 12 to 24 percent increase in the units per strip.

Figure 1: Strip density by size and block size

Figure 2: Count increase by package size for 4 to 2 block mold

The limitations extending beyond 100x300 are well known by the packaging industry, but getting a large scale effort by suppliers to invest will be the initial hurdle to overcome. When retooling established plating lines, competition on strip cost tends to constrain width growth.

As lead frame packages continue to become thinner, the capability to handle wider lead frames becomes more difficult. Becoming both wider and thinner, the strip's ability to handle die mass will decrease, causing the strip to bow during handling operations.

The current solution to handling wide/thin lead frame is to add rigidity and stability to the materials during handling by applying a backside tape, but the handling tapes must sustain high manufacturing temperatures needed for bake operations. The added handling requirement is a roadblock in the industry progression toward wider formats.

The last consideration is the new path of panel processing that is emerging. How will this new development relate to lead frame packages? It is strongly believed that at some point there will be an intersection of the technologies to enable roadmap discussions with equipment vendors and suppliers for all major lead frame products as more efficient panel processing and adequate technology will exist to enable wider lead frame panels to replace current lead frame technology.

Equipment and process inputs to lead frame advancements

From a lead frame packaging standpoint, the majority of processes and assembly methods are the same and follow the same production flow regardless of lead frame width. The key is in the equipment's capability to handle the width input.

Die attach, wire bond, dicing and visual processes are individual die handling processes that are limited today by the single filing method of processing an individual component through the operation. Wider lead frames at these processing steps contributes cost savings only during load/unload times through the efficiency of handling less strips and visual alignments to obtain the same output. This reduces the equipment need on a small scale and contributes to less processing cost overall at these assembly operations.

The only key challenge is strip width that in die attach, dicing and visual processes can be overcome by Updating rail designs and dimensions to accommodate the 100x300mm format can overcome the challenge of strip widths as chips run through the die attach, dicing and visual processes. In most cases, even wider formats can easily be updated if the equipment base's internal dimensions will allow rail increases.

A variety of handling devices are available to handle moving lead frames in and out of each manufacturing process, including inspection. An inspection handling jig can be inserted into the cassette to allow smooth transfer of the lead frame from the carrier (Fig. 3). Once in the fixture, the jig can be moved under microscopes and ring lights without any flexing or damage to the components.

Figure 4 shows before and after pictures of a cassette support fixture. This feature is incorporated in between each lead frame slot to provide bottom support and allow a rest area to avoid sagging or collapsing. This format has extended testing to 120x330mm with no problems in sag or handling at critical manual processes used on a large scale basis.

Figure 3: Strip inspection jig

Figure 4: Before/after cassette sag with fixture

When looking at wire bond processing equipment limitations driven by a need for capability versus speed of wire bonding, we must look at wire bonding processing equipment limitations. Increasing head travel distance degrades capability and, in large movements, can enable much slower outputs from the equipment. There are also complications with loop profile needs being eliminated with implementation of wider frame widths or table movement needs for wide lead frames. This has been a major source of restriction on a transition to wide lead frame and panel development. Currently, the only path to wider formats is to position wire bonding equipments face to face and utilize half strip bonding on each system. This enables wider lead frames but adds additional cycle time constraints for loading and unloading lead frames into the cycle.

Epoxy cure, deflash and mold are batch type processes that leverage the strip density and are impacted with the addition of wider more dense lead frames during processing. The differences in the coefficients of thermal expansion (CTE) when curing the die attach material create mold flash, voids and warpage issues.

The other challenge is finding a mold compound and process set up that will eliminate voiding and flow issues during the traditional mold type processes for block mold. Figure 5 shows single block transfer type molding and flow marks from mold compound curing before completely filled. This is a significant challenge moving forward as voiding, sweep and warpage will create process problems. Progression to new mold technologies is an option but should be considered last as it adds more equipment and materials requirements due to the delivery format of the mold compound. Figure 6 shows typical warpage from transfer mold. Mold compound selection is critical to reduce or minimize CTE mismatch leading to warpage that restricts handling and packaging offset issues at the package saw operation.

Figure 5: Strip warpage with correct mold choice

Figure 6: Strip mold flow marks from premature cure

The future of wide lead frame formats

Equipment vendors and lead frame suppliers must stay ahead of the industry needs and understand the roadmap for manufacturing equipment as we progress to wider forms. In this article, today's key limiting features for manufacturing factories was discussed to encourage active communication with suppliers on enabling the capability to drive innovation. Manufacturers must make sure the equipment purchased today will sustain advancements in width and needs for the factories of tomorrow.

Wide lead frame and equipment selection will continue to be a key challenge area for the industry for years to come. As we progress into possible alternatives of panel processing and higher densities, the same issues will exist. We must assure the current factory tooling will enable future advancements.
